## 1. Lieberman-Aiden et al. — Hi-C (Science 2009)

**Core concept:** Hi-C is the first method to map genome-wide chromatin contacts at megabase resolution, revealing that the human genome is partitioned into two distinct spatial compartments (A and B) corresponding to open, gene-rich regions versus closed, gene-poor regions — a "plaid pattern" of co-regulated loci visible as a contact matrix heatmap.

**Text analogy:** A corpus of texts has the same dual-compartment structure: some passages functionally co-occur, cross-reference, and mutually activate each other (Compartment A — active, accessible loci), while others are consistently isolated, suppressed, or peripheral to the main discourse (Compartment B — heterochromatic, lamina-associated). Hi-C's contact matrix — a pairwise co-occurrence frequency table — maps directly onto a document-term or passage-to-passage co-citation matrix. The "plaid pattern" Lieberman-Aiden observed when loci are sorted by their first eigenvector is identical in principle to what emerges when passages are sorted by a leading PCA component computed from their lexical similarity graph.

**Technical detail:** The core algorithm divides the genome into fixed-size bins, builds a raw contact count matrix M where M[i][j] = co-occurrence frequency, normalizes by expected contact at genomic distance, then computes a Pearson correlation matrix C on row profiles. The first eigenvector of C assigns each bin to compartment A or B. For text: divide a work into fixed-length segments, build M[i][j] = lexical co-occurrence or citation frequency, normalize by expected co-occurrence at narrative distance, extract the first eigenvector. Positive values = thematically active segments, negative = thematically latent.

**Key finding:** "The plaid pattern suggests that each chromosome can be decomposed into two sets of loci (arbitrarily labeled A and B) such that contacts within each set are enriched and contacts between sets are depleted." 71% of resulting matrix entries represent statistically significant correlations (P ≤ 0.05). The correlation matrix C is computed as C[i,j] = Pearson(row_i(M*), row_j(M*)).

**Development implication:** Palimpsest should implement a TextHiC module: sliding-window or chapter-level segment × segment co-occurrence matrix, Pearson-normalize it, extract leading eigenvectors, and assign each segment to a "thematic compartment." This gives a principled, unsupervised decomposition of any text into active vs. latent discourse layers — directly analogous to A/B compartments. The heatmap visualization (hot = frequent contact, cold = avoidance) is immediately meaningful to literary critics as a "thematic proximity map."

## 2. Dixon et al. — Topological Domains (Nature 2012)

**Core concept:** The genome is organized into discrete megabase-scale "topological associating domains" (TADs) — self-interacting chromatin blocks whose boundaries are conserved across cell types and species. Dixon identified 2,200 TADs in mouse ESCs (median size 880 kb), using a "directionality index" (DI) computed from Hi-C contact asymmetry and a Hidden Markov Model to call boundary positions.

**Text analogy:** TADs map directly onto the intuition of "chapters," "narrative arcs," or "thematic units" in a text — self-contained regions of high internal cohesion separated by sharp transition zones. The DI — measuring whether a region preferentially interacts with what comes before or after it — is a computational analogue for topic segmentation: does the current passage look more like what precedes it, or what follows? TAD boundaries correspond to scene breaks, tonal shifts, or rhetorical pivots. Crucially, Dixon showed that TAD boundaries are stable across cell types (conserved), suggesting they correspond to intrinsic structural features of the text rather than reader-dependent interpretation.

**Technical detail:** The directionality index DI for bin i is DI = (D - U) / |D - U| * ((D - E)^2/E + (U - E)^2/E), where D = downstream contacts within 2 Mb, U = upstream contacts within 2 Mb, E = expected = (D+U)/2. This chi-square-like statistic strongly positive means the region pulls right; negative means it pulls left. A HMM with states (upstream bias / downstream bias / no bias) then segments the DI signal into domains. For text: replace "contacts" with lexical similarity to subsequent vs. preceding windows; apply HMM segmentation to the resulting signal.

**Key finding:** 52% of the mouse genome has a DI that is non-random (FDR = 1%); 2,200 TADs with median 880 kb occupy ~91% of the genome; CTCF binding is strongly enriched at boundaries. Critically: "96% of dynamic interacting regions occur within the same topological domain" — meaning cross-boundary interactions are functionally rare, validating domains as meaningful units.

**Development implication:** Palimpsest should implement a DirectionalityIndex-based text segmentation algorithm: compute for each passage how much its vocabulary looks backward vs. forward, use a HMM to identify the underlying "domain" states. This provides a purely structural (non-topic-model-based) segmentation that is robust across different texts. The boundary-enrichment of CTCF (an insulator protein) maps to the literary concept of structural markers (chapter headings, white space, rhetorical pivots) — Palimpsest can use punctuation density, sentence length variance, or named-entity boundaries as "CTCF analogs" to validate computationally detected TAD-like boundaries.

## 3. Rao et al. — Kilobase-Resolution 3D Maps (Cell 2014)

**Core concept:** In situ Hi-C at kilobase resolution reveals 10,000 chromatin loops across nine human cell types, identifies 185-kb-median "contact domains," and shows that loop anchors predominantly occur in convergent CTCF-binding orientations (>90% facing each other) — establishing a directional, strand-oriented looping grammar for genome folding.

**Text analogy:** Loops connecting promoters and enhancers across megabase distances are the genomic analogue of long-range narrative callbacks — where a text element much later in a work (an enhancer) activates or recontextualizes a passage far upstream (a promoter). The convergent CTCF orientation rule maps to a textual insight: long-range coherence is not random but follows a directional grammar — a callback works when the referring passage "faces" the original, i.e., they share orientational or semantic polarity. The 10,000 loops that link promoters and enhancers to produce gene activation are analogous to the network of allusions, callbacks, and thematic echoes in a densely intertextual work.

**Technical detail:** Rao used a "Chromatin Loop Calling" algorithm (HiCCUPS) that identifies "peak pixels" — positions in the Hi-C matrix where contact enrichment is significantly above the local background estimated from four neighborhoods (horizontal, vertical, lower-left, donut). The enrichment is quantified as a q-value across all four local backgrounds. For text: treat a passage-pair similarity matrix as a Hi-C map; a "loop" is a passage pair (i, j) where their similarity is significantly above what is expected given their similarity to neighboring passages — i.e., a specific long-range thematic connection that cannot be explained by general topic proximity.

**Key finding:** "CTCF sites at loop anchors occur predominantly (>90%) in a convergent orientation, with the asymmetric motifs 'facing' one another." Loops contain 4.9 billion contacts at 1 kb resolution. Contact domains have median length 185 kb and associate with six distinct histone mark subcompartments.

**Development implication:** Palimpsest should build a LoopCaller: after computing a passage-pair similarity matrix, identify statistically anomalous long-range pairs where similarity is significantly higher than expected from both passages' general thematic neighborhood. These are candidate "narrative loops" — specific, directed echoes. The convergent-orientation finding suggests a further refinement: check whether the two passages in a loop are semantically "facing" (one introduces a concept, the other resolves it), which could be operationalized via entailment or topic-transition direction.

## 4. Bonev & Cavalli — 3D Genome Review (Nature Reviews Genetics 2016)

**Core concept:** This review synthesizes the layered hierarchy of chromatin organization — from nucleosome-level fiber structure through kilobase loops, TADs, compartments, and whole chromosome territories — and argues that 3D architecture is neither static nor deterministic but a dynamic interplay between CTCF/cohesin-mediated looping, loop extrusion, and transcriptional state, with structural features serving as a robust scaffold for context-dependent gene regulation.

**Text analogy:** Bonev & Cavalli's key insight is that genome organization operates simultaneously at multiple scales, each with different stability properties: chromosome territories (very stable, cell-type invariant) > compartments (stable, correlated with transcriptional activity) > TADs (stable across cell types) > individual loops (dynamic, cell-type specific). This directly maps to how literary structure works at multiple scales: genre conventions and macro-plot shapes are highly stable across versions; chapter/section architecture is moderately stable; paragraph-to-paragraph thematic flow is dynamic. Palimpsest needs a multi-scale architecture where analysis at each level uses appropriate tools and has distinct stability expectations.

**Technical detail:** The loop extrusion model — where cohesin "zips" along chromatin until blocked by convergently oriented CTCF sites — provides a dynamic mechanism that explains both TAD formation and loop specificity. The computational analog is a "sliding-window coherence" model: a scanning process that maximizes intra-window similarity until hitting a boundary signal, forming coherent text blocks bounded by structural discontinuities. Bonev & Cavalli note that only 15% of CTCF sites are at TAD boundaries, meaning most insulator-like signals are within-domain, creating sub-TAD structure — analogous to sub-chapter organization.

**Key finding:** "Only 15% of all mammalian CTCF-binding sites are located within a boundary; the majority lie within TADs and are thought to be involved in intra-TAD interactions." CTCF knockdown in human cell lines "did not strongly affect TAD boundaries but decreased intra-domain interactions and increased inter-domain contacts" — showing that boundary formation and intra-domain organization are mechanistically separable processes.

**Development implication:** Palimpsest should implement a strict hierarchy of analysis levels with explicit stability assumptions: macro-level compartment analysis (A/B decomposition, most stable) > TAD-level segmentation (moderately stable, use DI + HMM) > loop-level specific callbacks (dynamic, cell-type/text-specific). The loop extrusion model suggests a text-scanning algorithm: move a coherence window across the text, extending it until a sharp boundary signal is encountered (low similarity to next passage + presence of structural marker), then lock that TAD. This top-down constraint is more principled than k-means or topic models.

## 5. ENCODE Project Consortium — Phase II (Nature 2012)

**Core concept:** ENCODE systematically mapped 1,640 functional datasets across the human genome — transcription factor binding (636,336 regions in 119 factors), DNase hypersensitivity sites (2.89 million), histone modifications (12 marks across 46 cell types), and DNA methylation — assigning biochemical functions to 80.4% of the genome. Seven chromatin states partition the genome into 399,124 enhancer-like and 70,292 promoter-like regions, among others.

**Text analogy:** ENCODE's core contribution is the demonstration that "function" in the genome is not binary (coding/noncoding) but a multidimensional chromatin state — defined by the combinatorial pattern of multiple marks at each locus. A passage in a text is similarly not simply "important" or "not important" but carries a multidimensional annotation: rhetorical role (promoter-like if it introduces a theme, enhancer-like if it amplifies one from a distance), structural accessibility (is it heavily cited/referenced — DNase-accessible — or rarely engaged?), and modification state (heavily glossed by later scholars, thematically repressed, transitional). ENCODE's 7-state chromatin model maps to a literary "passage state" classifier.

**Technical detail:** ENCODE's chromatin state model uses a multivariate HMM (ChromHMM) where each genomic position is assigned to a hidden state based on the combinatorial pattern of 5-12 binary histone marks. Each state has a distinct emission probability vector for the marks, and states are inferred by Baum-Welch EM. For text: replace histone marks with textual features (tf-idf prominence, citation frequency by downstream passages, syntactic complexity, sentiment, named entity density, novel vocabulary introduction rate) and train a multivariate HMM to learn latent "passage states" from their feature co-occurrence patterns.

**Key finding:** "95% of the genome lies within 8 kilobases of a DNA-protein interaction, and 99% is within 1.7 kb of at least one of the biochemical events measured by ENCODE." This is the "most of the genome is functional" result — formerly called junk DNA. For texts: most of a text's passages contribute some functional signal, even if it is not the primary semantic content; the challenge is characterizing what kind of function, not whether there is function.

**Development implication:** Palimpsest should implement a ChromHMM-equivalent: a multivariate HMM trained on multiple simultaneous textual feature tracks (lexical, syntactic, structural, intertextual) to assign each passage to one of N latent "functional states." This is more powerful than single-feature segmentation because it captures combinatorial logic: a passage that is lexically sparse but syntactically complex and heavily cross-referenced is in a distinct state from one that is lexically rich but structurally isolated. The 7-state model (promoter-active, enhancer-active, transcribed, repressed, bivalent, heterochromatic, quiescent) is a ready-made starting vocabulary for literary annotation.

## 6. Roadmap Epigenomics Consortium — 111 Reference Epigenomes (Nature 2015)

**Core concept:** The Roadmap generates the largest reference collection of human epigenomes (111 primary cells and tissues), profiling five histone marks across all samples and deriving a 15-state chromatin model that is trained jointly, enabling systematic comparison of regulatory landscapes across cell types. The key advance over ENCODE is the discovery of tissue-specific regulatory modules: enhancers active in only one tissue type, linked to tissue-specific gene expression programs and GWAS disease-associated variants.

**Text analogy:** The 111-epigenome framework maps to a corpus of the same text across 111 contexts: different editions, translations, critical reception periods, reader communities, or genres. Just as the Roadmap identifies which regulatory elements are constitutive (shared across all cell types) versus tissue-specific (active only in brain, or heart), Palimpsest can identify which textual features are constitutive (appearing in all versions/readings of a work) versus context-specific (emerging only in certain editions, translations, or critical traditions). The "reference epigenome" concept suggests building canonical annotation tracks for a text that represent its stable, consensus functional profile, on top of which context-specific deviations are registered.

**Technical detail:** The Roadmap's 15-state model uses ChromHMM trained jointly across all 111 epigenomes with a shared state vocabulary but cell-type-specific emission parameters. States are identified by their average histone mark combination and their DNA methylation and accessibility levels. For text: train a joint HMM on a corpus of related texts (or the same text across editions) so that the state vocabulary is shared but emission probabilities can vary by text, enabling cross-text comparison of passage-state assignments.

**Key finding:** Active enhancer and promoter states "covered approximately 5% of each reference epigenome on average" and showed "enrichment for evolutionarily conserved non-exonic regions." 96% of CpGs showed differential methylation in at least one cell type — meaning regulatory context is nearly universally context-dependent, not a fixed property. The 15-state model: TssA (active TSS), TssAFlnk (flanking TSS), TxFlnk (transcribed flanking), Tx (strong transcription), TxWk (weak transcription), Enh (enhancer), EnhG (genic enhancer), ZNF/Rpts, Het (heterochromatin), TssBiv, BivFlnk, EnhBiv, ReprPC, ReprPCWk, Quies.

**Development implication:** Palimpsest should build a reference annotation pipeline: given a canonical version of a text, learn a shared 15-state passage model across multiple readings/editions/translations and use it to build a "reference epigenome" for the work — a consensus annotation that can be compared against any new reading or edition. Passages that are consistently in the same state across all contexts (like constitutive promoters) are the stable anchors; those that flip states (like tissue-specific enhancers) are the interpretively contested zones. This gives literary scholars a principled way to locate where meaning is fixed vs. contested.

## 7. Barabasi & Oltvai — Network Biology (Nature Reviews Genetics 2004)

**Core concept:** Most biological molecular interaction networks — metabolic, protein-protein, transcriptional regulatory — are scale-free: their degree distribution follows a power law P(k) ~ k^(-γ) with γ ≈ 2-3, meaning a small number of highly connected hub nodes dominate connectivity. These networks also show hierarchical modularity (high clustering coefficient at low degree, decreasing with degree), making them simultaneously robust to random node failure but vulnerable to targeted hub removal.

**Text analogy:** A literary corpus or a single complex text is a scale-free network in multiple senses: a small number of concepts, characters, or terms appear enormously frequently (hubs — Hamlet, "death," "time") while most appear rarely; a small number of passages are densely referenced while most are peripheral. This is not merely a metaphor — empirical analysis of character co-occurrence networks in novels consistently yields power-law degree distributions. The hub vulnerability principle maps to literary criticism: removing the hub concept from a text's semantic network ("death" from Hamlet) catastrophically changes the network's meaning-organization, whereas removing a peripheral term is invisible.

**Technical detail:** Scale-free topology is identified by fitting the degree distribution on a log-log plot: if P(k) = k^(-γ) is linear on log-log axes, the network is scale-free. The Barabasi-Albert preferential attachment model generates scale-free networks by a simple growth rule: new nodes attach to existing nodes with probability proportional to their current degree ("rich get richer"). For text: the preferential attachment model maps to how concepts accumulate citations — terms already highly cross-referenced attract more cross-references. The clustering coefficient C(k) scaling as k^(-1) (hierarchical networks) identifies the multi-scale modular structure.

**Key finding:** "Most biological networks are scale-free, which means that their degree distribution follows a power law." The metabolic networks of 43 organisms from all three domains of life show scale-free topology. The degree exponent for biological networks is typically γ ∈ [2, 3]. Hierarchical network structure: C(k) ~ k^(-1), meaning highly connected nodes link modules that are themselves internally dense.

**Development implication:** Palimpsest should build a network layer for every text: nodes are passages or semantic units, edges are similarity scores or direct allusions, and the first analysis should characterize network topology (degree distribution, clustering, average path length). If scale-free (expected for rich texts), the hub passages should be identified and elevated as structural anchors in the visualization. The Barabasi-Albert preferential attachment model can be used to generate synthetic "null texts" against which real texts are compared: does the allusion network of Paradise Lost show the scale-free, hub-and-spoke structure expected, or is it more uniform? Deviations from scale-free topology in a text signal intentional structural choices by the author.

## 8. Senft & Macfarlan — Transposable Elements (Nature Reviews Genetics 2021)

**Core concept:** Transposable elements (TEs) — which make up ~50% of mammalian genomes — are not genomic parasites but active contributors to regulatory innovation: they provide species-specific enhancer sequences, promoters, and non-coding RNAs that have been co-opted by the host to drive mammalian-specific developmental programs, particularly placentation. The arms race between TEs and host KRAB-ZFP repressors generates continuous regulatory novelty.

**Text analogy:** TEs are the analogue of literary borrowings, quotations, formulaic phrases, genre conventions, and intertextual citations — sequences that originate outside a text (from prior works, oral traditions, translation sources, common rhetorical repertoires) but become integrated into the host text's regulatory logic. Just as TEs are initially silenced (by KRAB-ZFPs) but then co-opted as enhancers, borrowed phrases are initially foreign (marked as citations, italicized) but become naturalized into the text's own voice. The 80% of mouse species-specific TSC enhancers derived from TEs maps to the finding that much of what seems most distinctively "original" in a text derives from co-opted external material. Crucially, TE expression patterns are "so specific that they can stage embryos, determine evolutionary relationships, label cancer cell types" — borrowed language can similarly fingerprint a text's literary genealogy with high precision.

**Technical detail:** TE analysis uses RepeatMasker to annotate TE families in genomic sequence, then ChIP-seq or ATAC-seq to assess which TE instances show active regulatory marks in specific cell types. Co-option is identified by overlap between TE coordinates and active enhancer states (H3K27ac, chromatin accessibility). For text: use a plagiarism-detection or sequence alignment algorithm (Smith-Waterman, or BLAST-equivalent for text — e.g., MinHash/LSH) to identify all exogenous sequences embedded in a text; then assess which embedded sequences have been "activated" (extensively developed, thematically central) vs. "silenced" (present but peripheral, unelaborated). The KRAB-ZFP/TE arms race maps to the tension between intertextual citation (acknowledgment, domestication) and unmarked borrowing (suppressed TE-like integration).

**Key finding:** "80% of these species-specific putative TSC enhancers are derived from species-specific TEs, especially LTRs." TEs "can stage embryos, determine evolutionary relationships, label cancer cell types, predict disease outcome and characterize neurodegenerative disease." RLTR13D5 elements (mouse-specific ERVs) provide over 300 putative trophectoderm enhancers co-bound by CDX2, ELF5, and EOMES.

**Development implication:** Palimpsest should implement an Intertextual TE Detector: given a text, identify all passages that are structurally derived from a reference corpus (using sequence alignment or dense semantic similarity to known prior texts), classify them by TE family analog (direct quotation = DNA transposon, approximate echo = SINE, transformed allusion = processed pseudogene), and then assess their regulatory state (are they thematically active hubs, or silenced background?). The KRAB-ZFP analogy suggests that texts with strong authorial voice show active "silencing" of their borrowed material — the source is suppressed while the regulatory function is retained.

## 9. Bartel — MicroRNAs (Cell 2009)

**Core concept:** MicroRNAs are ~23 nt non-coding RNAs that repress protein-coding genes by pairing to the 3' UTR of target mRNAs, primarily via a 7-8 nt "seed" match at miRNA positions 2-8. A single miRNA family can repress hundreds of targets simultaneously; most regulatory targets have only a single seed match; and the seed mechanism is sufficiently constrained that a simple three-step search (identify two 7-mers, find conserved orthologs, search UTRs) predicts authentic targets with a 3.5:1 signal-to-noise ratio.

**Text analogy:** MiRNAs are the analogue of recurring micro-motifs — short, highly conserved phrase patterns, syntactic constructions, or semantic seeds — that simultaneously modulate hundreds of downstream passages. A 7-8 word phrase pattern ("seed") can act as a regulatory signal that, when present in a passage's "3' UTR" (its conclusion or transitional zone), identifies that passage as under the control of a thematic motif. The key insight is that target recognition is minimal and combinatorial: a short seed is sufficient; most targets have only one seed match; and the same seed (miRNA family = motif family) controls all members. This is directly analogous to how a recurring syntactic pattern (anaphora, chiasmus, a particular meter) can simultaneously govern the feel and meaning of hundreds of dispersed passages.

**Technical detail:** The three-step protocol for miRNA target prediction: (1) identify the two 7-mer matches to miRNA positions 2-8 (seed hexamer + either A at position 1 [7mer-A1] or a match at position 8 [7mer-m8]); (2) compile orthologous sequences from multiple genomes; (3) search for conserved instances. For text analogy: (1) extract a candidate micro-motif (6-8 words or a syntactic pattern); (2) compile the same text across multiple editions or translations; (3) find passages where the motif is conserved across versions — conservation = functional constraint = genuine regulatory role. An 8-mer match gives higher specificity; a 6-mer gives higher sensitivity, exactly as in text analysis where longer patterns are more specific but rarer.

**Key finding:** "Requiring conserved Watson-Crick pairing to the 5' region of the miRNA centered on nucleotides 2-7, which is called the miRNA 'seed,' markedly reduces the occurrence of false-positive predictions." For a five-genome analysis including chicken, the ratio of predicted targets to estimated false positives is 3.5:1 for 7-mer seed matches. Members of the same miRNA family (same positions 2-8) share the same predicted target set regardless of other sequence differences.

**Development implication:** Palimpsest should build a MicroMotif detector: identify short (6-8 unit) recurring syntactic or lexical patterns in a text, then use a conservation-based filter (do these patterns recur at structurally parallel positions in related texts, other works by the same author, or across translations?) to distinguish functional motifs from coincidental repetition. The seed logic — positions 2-7 matter most, not 1 or 8+ — suggests that the interior of a phrase carries more regulatory information than its exact endpoints, which maps to linguists' finding that function words at phrase boundaries are more variable than content cores. A "miRNA family" in literary terms is a set of surface-distinct phrases that share the same 6-mer interior seed and therefore co-regulate the same set of target passages.

## 10. Barabasi — Network Science Textbook (Cambridge, 2016, pp. 1-50)

**Core concept:** Network science provides universal quantitative tools — degree distribution, clustering coefficient, average path length, community structure, robustness, and spreading dynamics — applicable to any system that can be represented as nodes and links. The field's core discovery is that real networks (biological, social, technological, informational) are more similar to each other than to random graphs, being governed by shared organizing principles including scale-free degree distributions and small-world properties.

**Text analogy:** The textbook's pedagogical framework is itself an argument for Palimpsest: any literary text or corpus can be cast as a network whose nodes are passages, characters, concepts, or words, and whose links are co-occurrence, allusion, discourse reference, or semantic similarity. The textbook's five key measurements — N (nodes), L (links), degree distribution, average path length, clustering coefficient — provide the minimum viable characterization of any text-network, giving researchers a common quantitative vocabulary across very different texts and corpora. The "networks at the heart of complex systems" argument applies directly: complex literary works (Paradise Lost, Ulysses) are governed by the same organizing principles as other complex systems.

**Technical detail:** The minimum-viable network analysis protocol from chapter 1: measure N (number of nodes), L (number of links), average degree <k> = 2L/N, degree distribution P(k), clustering coefficient C (probability that two neighbors of a node are themselves connected), average path length l. For networks with power-law P(k) ~ k^(-γ), fit γ using maximum likelihood or log-log regression. For a literary text-network: N = number of distinct semantic units (passages, characters, concepts), L = number of co-occurring or cross-referencing pairs, <k> = average number of connections per passage. Community detection algorithms (Louvain, Girvan-Newman) identify thematic modules.

**Key finding:** "Despite the obvious diversity of complex systems, the structure and the evolution of the networks behind each system is driven by a common set of fundamental laws and principles." The preface states the course covers: random networks (Erdos-Renyi), scale-free property, Barabasi-Albert preferential attachment model, degree correlations, network robustness, communities, and spreading phenomena. The vulnerability-due-to-interconnectivity theme — local failures propagating globally — maps to a specific literary phenomenon: when a structural hub is disrupted (a key allusion is misread, a pivotal chapter is removed), global meaning cascades.

**Development implication:** Palimpsest should use the Network Science textbook's pedagogy as a curriculum for its analytical pipeline — implementing each chapter's core measurement as a module: (Ch. 2) Basic graph representation of texts; (Ch. 3) Test whether text-networks are random or non-random; (Ch. 4) Identify scale-free degree distribution and compute γ; (Ch. 5) Simulate preferential attachment to model text growth/influence; (Ch. 8) Network robustness — which passages are essential vs. redundant?; (Ch. 9) Community detection — identify thematic modules; (Ch. 10) Spreading dynamics — how does a motif or allusion propagate through a text network over time or across a corpus. This gives Palimpsest a complete, principled quantitative toolbox grounded in the most rigorous available network theory.

## Cross-Paper Synthesis for Palimpsest Architecture

The ten papers collectively suggest a four-layer architecture:

**Layer 1 — Contact Map** (Lieberman-Aiden + Rao): Build pairwise passage similarity matrices at multiple resolutions; normalize by expected proximity; extract A/B compartments via eigenvector decomposition; call specific long-range "loops" as anomalous high-similarity pairs.

**Layer 2 — Domain Segmentation** (Dixon + Bonev & Cavalli): Apply directionality index + HMM to segment the contact map into TAD-like thematic domains; validate boundaries against structural markers (CTCF analogs in typography/syntax); accept that boundaries are stable but intra-domain interactions are dynamic.

**Layer 3 — Functional Annotation** (ENCODE + Roadmap): Apply multivariate ChromHMM-equivalent to assign each passage to one of 15 functional states based on its combinatorial feature profile; build reference epigenomes for canonical texts enabling cross-text and cross-edition comparison.

**Layer 4 — Network and Regulatory Logic** (Barabasi & Oltvai + Barabasi textbook + Bartel + Senft): Overlay a scale-free network on the contact map; identify hubs; detect co-opted intertextual elements (TE analogs); identify seed-motifs that simultaneously regulate large passage sets.

The Roadmap's 111-epigenome framework is the long-term vision: a reference library of annotations across 111 literary works (or 111 readings of a single work), enabling comparative regulatory genomics of literature.
